# Waypoint Product Context

## Register

product

## Surface Notes

Waypoint includes an expressive marketing homepage, but default design decisions should serve the planning workspace: destinations, itinerary editing, schedule conflict handling, and budgeting.

## Product

Waypoint is a travel planning web app for turning destination ideas, multi-day schedules, activity details, and budget decisions into one composed planning desk.

## Primary users

Waypoint serves travelers planning for themselves and small groups. The core user may begin independently, then need to coordinate shared decisions, timing, and cost visibility with companions.

## User goals

- Compare and narrow destinations without losing the thread of the trip.
- Build a day-by-day itinerary with activities, timing, and movement between days.
- Notice conflicts early enough to make calm planning decisions.
- Understand budget categories and per-traveler costs without spreadsheet overhead.
- Keep the workspace readable, responsive, and usable across desktop and mobile.

## Brand personality

Editorial calm: polished, composed, and decision-oriented. The interface should feel intentionally designed without drifting into luxury-template sameness or decorative travel nostalgia.

## Strategic design principles

- Product clarity wins over decoration. Every visual choice should make planning, comparison, editing, or review easier.
- Use the marketing surface to establish confidence, then let app surfaces become quieter and more task-forward.
- Preserve a sense of craft through typography, spacing, and hierarchy rather than postcard visuals or ornamental travel motifs.
- Prefer visible system state: conflicts, empty states, validation, and saved changes should be clear without being loud.
- Design for solo-to-small-group planning: support both personal decisions and shared-plan legibility.

## Anti-references

- Generic travel clichés: postcard collages, airport-board nostalgia, beige scrapbook paper, overused map pins, passport stamps, and luxury-concierge tropes.
- SaaS sameness: hero metric blocks, endless icon-card grids, gradient text, glass cards, and decorative dashboards that do not support the task.
- Overly whimsical travel UI that makes serious timing or budget decisions feel unserious.
- Low-contrast muted text or placeholder-heavy screens that look calm but become hard to read.

## Constraints

- Meet WCAG AA contrast expectations for body text and interactive controls.
- Keep keyboard navigation, visible focus states, and reduced-motion support intact.
- Avoid category clichés even when adding delight or marketing polish.
- Treat app surfaces as production UI, not a concept mockup: loading, empty, error, success, validation, and conflict states matter.
